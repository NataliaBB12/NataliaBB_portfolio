## Context

Stori gives access to a credit card to users who have been historically excluded from the formal financial system — helping them start or repair their credit history through a fully digital experience.

The app focuses on financial inclusion and education, turning abstract financial concepts into simple, actionable guidance that fits everyday life. This project brought that education and clarity directly into the core payment experience.

**Collaborators:** Luis Cota (Product Lead) · Carlos Martínez (Tech Lead)

---

## Problem Statement

> "Users weren't paying on time — not because they didn't want to, but because the experience made it hard to know when to pay, how much to pay, and why it mattered."

<InsightCard label="HMW" accent>
  **How might we increase the time-to-decision for payment options — so users always know exactly what to do next?**
</InsightCard>

## Goals to Achieve

<InsightCard label="Business OKR — >85% Pay-on-Time Rate">
  Increase POT rate above 85%, reducing delinquency and lowering support contact rate.
</InsightCard>

<InsightCard label="Business Goal — Single Source of Truth">
  Create one consistent source of truth for payment states across the entire app — simplifying dates, amounts, and statuses so users always know what to do next.

<InsightCard label="User Goal — Clarity & Confidence">

## Research Insights

We analyzed product data, internal surveys with the collections team, app store reviews, and social feedback to identify the real blockers — not the assumed ones.

<InsightCard label="01 — Confusion About How Credit Works">
  50%+ of users didn't understand billing cycles. 44% of support tickets asked: "How long does my payment take to show?" — users called support instead of trusting the app.
</InsightCard>

<InsightCard label="02 — App Access Was Blocking Payments Entirely">
  40% of open tickets were access-related. KYC and login friction acted as an invisible payment barrier — even a perfect payment experience fails if the user can't reach it.
</InsightCard>

<InsightCard label="03 — Payments Were a Manual, Repetitive Burden">
  30% of users asked for installment or deferred payment options. 10% said reminders would make a real difference. Every month, users had to reconstruct the same flow from scratch.
</InsightCard>

## Challenges

### Complexity Hiding in Plain Sight
The billing cycle is genuinely complex. Our job wasn't to hide it — it was to make it legible without oversimplifying in a way that broke trust.

### Access as a Silent Blocker
Improving payment UX meant we couldn't ignore login and KYC. Payment design had to account for imperfect entry points, not assume a perfect start.

### Behavior Change, Not Just UI Change
Reducing friction wasn't enough. Users also needed a reason to care — connecting on-time payments to real credit growth was part of the design work.

### One Truth, Everywhere
Payment states and messages were inconsistent across the app. We had to create a single source of truth without disrupting a live product at scale.

---

## Design Process

We worked iteratively — targeting specific parts of the payment journey, validating impact, and building toward a clearer future vision one step at a time.

1. **Discover** — Analyzed product queries, Quicksight dashboards, and internal surveys with the collections team to understand where and why users were falling off the payment path.

2. **Listen** — Synthesized app store reviews and social channel feedback to capture unfiltered user frustrations — surface-level language that revealed real pain points.

3. **Define** — Mapped the full payment journey from app access to confirmation, identifying where confusion, friction, and drop-off lived. Framed three clear design opportunities.

4. **Ideate** — Ran ideation sessions to explore solutions for each opportunity — date visualization, status clarity, direct debit — then prioritized based on data impact and feasibility.

5. **Build** — Designed, tested, and refined across multiple iterations — using data to validate or challenge each design hypothesis before moving forward.

6. **Ship** — Worked closely with Product and Engineering to ship features in focused, measurable increments — maintaining alignment between design intent and built reality throughout.

## User Feedback

> "How could you help me pay off my total balance without generating interest on my Stori card?"

The question behind the question — users weren't asking for less debt, they were asking for a payment experience that worked with them, not against them.

<InsightCard label="84% — App Check-In for Payment Clarity">
  84% of users were already opening the app to know how much and when to pay. They wanted to pay — the design just needed to meet them there.
</InsightCard>

<InsightCard label="30% — Want Installment or Deferred Options">
  Nearly a third of users wanted more flexible payment structures — a signal that one-size payment flows were leaving a significant segment behind.
</InsightCard>

<InsightCard label="12% — Rely on App Notifications">
  Only 12% relied on push notifications for payment timing — meaning most users were self-managing, and the in-app experience was the primary payment interface.
</InsightCard>

## Reflections

### Iterate in Small, Focused Steps
Rather than trying to fix everything at once, it worked better to target specific parts of the payment journey — dates, amounts, statuses — and validate each before moving on. Focused bets over big-bang redesigns.

### Behavior Change Needs Both Clarity and Motivation
Reducing friction isn't enough. People also need a reason to care. Connecting on-time payments to credit growth and real opportunities made the experience meaningful — not just functional.

### Early, Honest Communication Is Critical
In a fast-moving fintech environment, alignment between product, design, engineering, and collections is essential to move the needle without confusing users. Silence between teams always shows up in the product.

### Access and Payment Form One Journey
If users can't access the app reliably, they can't pay on time. Payment design must account for KYC and login constraints — not assume a perfect entry point every time.

### Data-Driven Hypotheses Save Time and Trust
Every design decision anchored in real data moved faster and landed better with stakeholders. Intuition opens doors — data keeps them open.
