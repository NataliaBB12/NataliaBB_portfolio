### What the Data Really Meant

<T en="**Users scan, they don't read.** Putting all critical explanations in a dense onboarding screen guarantees most users will miss them. Critical information needs to appear at the right step — not all at once upfront." es="**Los usuarios escanean, no leen.** Poner todas las explicaciones críticas en una pantalla de onboarding densa garantiza que la mayoría de los usuarios las pasará por alto. La información crítica debe aparecer en el paso correcto — no toda de golpe al inicio." />

<T en="**The real confusion is about consequences.** The core question wasn't 'what is prepayment?' It was: 'what happens to my next due date and amount if I prepay today?' Users needed a before/after view, not a definition." es="**La confusión real es sobre las consecuencias.** La pregunta central no era '¿qué es el prepago?' Era: '¿qué pasa con mi próxima fecha y monto de pago si prepago hoy?' Los usuarios necesitaban una vista de antes/después, no una definición." />

<T en="**Financial decisions need low cognitive load.** When people deal with debt and interest, they're more sensitive to information overload. Too much at once quickly becomes overwhelming — and hurts trust." es="**Las decisiones financieras necesitan poca carga cognitiva.** Cuando las personas lidian con deuda e intereses, son más sensibles a la sobrecarga de información. Demasiado a la vez se vuelve abrumador rápidamente — y daña la confianza." />

## Challenges

### #1 — Friction Right After Intent
<T en="Right after tapping the payment button, users hit a full-screen onboarding that tried to explain the entire prepayment concept at once. Instead of feeling helpful, this 'brake screen' interrupted the sense of immediacy and felt like a barrier — especially for users who just wanted to pay and move on." es="Justo después de tocar el botón de pago, los usuarios se topaban con un onboarding de pantalla completa que intentaba explicar todo el concepto de prepago de una vez. En lugar de sentirse útil, esta 'pantalla de freno' interrumpía la sensación de inmediatez y se sentía como una barrera — especialmente para los usuarios que solo querían pagar y continuar." />

### #2 — Critical Information in the Wrong Place
<T en="Most of the explanation about how prepayment works lived only in that initial onboarding. Because users scanned and dismissed it quickly, they entered the flow without understanding the key rules about dates and interest — setting up confusion for every step that followed." es="La mayor parte de la explicación sobre cómo funciona el prepago solo existía en ese onboarding inicial. Como los usuarios lo escaneaban y descartaban rápidamente, entraban al flujo sin entender las reglas clave sobre fechas e intereses — generando confusión en cada paso siguiente." />

### #3 — Only One Visible Journey
<T en="The interface exposed essentially one path for early payment, regardless of what the user actually wanted to do. This made it hard to compare options or choose which installments to advance — reinforcing the misconception that prepaying 'skips a month' rather than recalculating amounts and dates." es="La interfaz exponía esencialmente un solo camino para el pago anticipado, independientemente de lo que el usuario realmente quería hacer. Esto dificultaba comparar opciones o elegir qué cuotas adelantar — reforzando el malentendido de que prepagar 'salta un mes' en lugar de recalcular montos y fechas." />

<T en="**Design Principle Applied:** These problems are textbook examples of Hick's Law and high cognitive load — too many concepts thrown at users at once, in the wrong moment and format for a high-stakes financial decision." es="**Principio de Diseño Aplicado:** Estos problemas son ejemplos de manual de la Ley de Hick y alta carga cognitiva — demasiados conceptos lanzados al usuario a la vez, en el momento y formato equivocados para una decisión financiera de alto impacto." />

## Design Process

<T en="We worked within tight development constraints, prioritizing high-impact, low-effort improvements that could ship quickly — while still moving the needle on contact rate and NPS." es="Trabajamos dentro de restricciones de desarrollo ajustadas, priorizando mejoras de alto impacto y bajo esfuerzo que pudieran lanzarse rápidamente — sin dejar de mover la aguja en la tasa de contacto y el NPS." />

1. **Research** — Ran usability tests on the existing flow and analyzed product data to identify where and why users were getting confused or abandoning. Observed real behavior, not assumptions.

2. **Define** — Synthesized findings into three core UX problems. Validated user journeys against multiple "what if" scenarios to map edge cases and reduce the risk of future support tickets.

3. **Ideate** — Led a workshop with the broader team to explore different focus areas. Design critiques broadened the solution space and helped balance user clarity, business goals, and implementation cost.

4. **Design** — Applied progressive disclosure principles — distributing key information across steps instead of one dense screen. Redesigned the flow to show users a transparent before/after view of their installments.

5. **Validate** — Conducted early validations with the development team to ensure solutions were cost-effective to build. Ran usability tests on the redesigned flow — confirming users understood core mechanics better even if they didn't read every word.

6. **Ship** — First release focused on onboarding redesign, copy improvements, and critical flow steps — shipping changes incrementally so development could implement them without blocking other platform work.

## Solution Implemented

<T en="We reworked the entire user flow to make early payment feel transparent, step-by-step, and user-led — replacing a dense onboarding with contextual guidance delivered at the right moment." es="Rediseñamos todo el flujo de usuario para que el pago anticipado se sintiera transparente, paso a paso y guiado por el usuario — reemplazando un onboarding denso con orientación contextual entregada en el momento correcto." />

<InsightCard label="Progressive Disclosure of Payment Information" labelEs="Divulgación Progresiva de la Información de Pago" es="Las explicaciones sobre el ahorro en intereses y los cambios de fecha se distribuyeron en varios pasos en fragmentos más pequeños y contextuales — para que la información más importante aparezca cuando es realmente relevante, no toda de golpe al inicio.">
  Distributed explanations about interest savings and date changes across several steps in smaller, contextual chunks — so the most important information appears when it's actually relevant, not all at once upfront.
</InsightCard>

<InsightCard label="Transparent Before/After Installment View" labelEs="Vista Transparente Antes/Después de las Cuotas" es="Se mostró a los usuarios exactamente cómo el pago anticipado cambia sus cuotas — una vista comparativa que abordó directamente el malentendido de 'saltarse un mes' y lo reemplazó con una imagen clara de qué cambia y qué no.">
  Showed users exactly how paying early changes their installments — a side-by-side view that directly addressed the "skips a month" misconception and replaced it with a clear picture of what changes and what doesn't.
</InsightCard>

<InsightCard label="Manual Installment Selection" labelEs="Selección Manual de Cuotas" es="Se permitió a los usuarios elegir explícitamente qué cuota(s) quieren adelantar, de atrás hacia adelante. Esto aclaró que el prepago es una elección activa — no un cambio inesperado en su comportamiento de pago regular.">
  Let users explicitly choose which installment(s) they want to advance, from back to front. This clarified that prepayment is an active choice — not an unexpected change to their regular payment behavior.
</InsightCard>

<InsightCard label="Clear Confirmation & Summary Screen" labelEs="Pantalla de Confirmación y Resumen Clara" es="Se añadió una pantalla final que resume cuánto están pagando ahora, cuánto interés están ahorrando y cuál será su nueva próxima fecha y monto de vencimiento — para que los usuarios puedan confirmar con confianza, no con incertidumbre.">
  Added a final screen summarizing how much they're paying now, how much interest they're saving, and what their new next due date and amount will be — so users could confirm with confidence, not uncertainty.
</InsightCard>

## Reflections

### Communication Is Part of the Product
Clear, aligned communication between product, design, content, and stakeholders is essential when dealing with money and regulation. Copy and flows alone can't fix misaligned expectations at the team level.

### Hypotheses Before Shipping
When data is missing, it's better to make assumptions explicit and validate them step by step than to iterate blindly in production. Naming the assumption is half the work.

### Designing Within Constraints Is Still Designing
Technical and regulatory constraints forced prioritization of low-effort, high-impact improvements — copy, onboarding, flow steps — that could ship quickly while still moving the needle. Constraints are a creative brief, not a blocker.

### Mapping Edge Cases Builds Trust
Validating the new flow against multiple scenarios and "what if" cases helped prevent surprises for users and reduced the risk of future support tickets. Edge cases aren't edge cases for the people who hit them.

### Collaboration Amplifies Solutions
Workshops and design critiques with the team broadened the range of ideas and led to a solution that balanced user clarity, business goals, and implementation cost. The best ideas didn't come from a single designer working alone.

### Leading Workshops Expanded My Perspective
Being tasked with facilitating the brainstorming session gave me a broader lens on how to rethink solutions — and strengthened my ability to align a cross-functional team around a shared direction.
